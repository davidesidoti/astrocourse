Work, Energy, Power

Quick Check Assessment

Instructions

This quick check exercise covers the content from Section 3: Work, Energy, Power. Answer all questions to test your understanding of energy transformations and conservation in astronomical systems.

The assessment includes conceptual questions, mathematical problems, and applied astronomy scenarios. Complete all questions and click "Submit Answers" to see your results.

Conceptual Understanding Questions

Question 1 Multiple Choice (Select the best answer)

Which of the following best describes the relationship between work and energy in a physical system?

Explanation:

Work is the energy transferred to an object by applying a force through a displacement. Mathematically, work is calculated as W = F·d·cos(θ), where F is the force, d is the displacement, and θ is the angle between the force and displacement vectors. When work is done on an object, energy is transferred to that object, which can change its kinetic energy, potential energy, or both. This relationship is formalized in the work-energy theorem, which states that the net work done on an object equals the change in its kinetic energy.

The rate at which energy is transferred is power, not work. Work is the energy transfer itself, not the rate of transfer.

Question 2 Multiple Choice (Select all that apply)

Which of the following are forms of energy important in astronomical systems? (Check all that apply)

Explanation:

All five forms of energy listed are important in astronomical systems:

- Gravitational potential energy is crucial in orbital dynamics, star formation, and galaxy structure. It's the energy stored in the gravitational field between massive objects.

- Kinetic energy is the energy of motion, present in all moving astronomical objects from planets orbiting stars to galaxies moving through space.

- Thermal energy is the internal energy of a system due to the motion of its particles. It's essential in understanding stellar interiors, planetary atmospheres, and the interstellar medium.

- Electromagnetic energy includes all forms of electromagnetic radiation (light, radio waves, X-rays, etc.) that carry information across space and are crucial for astronomical observations.

- Nuclear energy powers stars through fusion reactions and is released in supernovae and other high-energy astrophysical events.

The interplay between these different forms of energy drives the evolution of astronomical systems at all scales.

Question 3 True/False

The total energy of an isolated system can change if work is done within the system, but the total energy plus work is always conserved.

Explanation:

This statement is false. According to the law of conservation of energy, the total energy of an isolated system remains constant; it can neither be created nor destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. Work done within an isolated system simply transfers energy from one part of the system to another or converts it between different forms, but it doesn't change the total energy of the system.

In astronomical contexts, this principle is crucial. For example, in a star-planet system, gravitational potential energy can be converted to kinetic energy as a planet falls toward its star, but the total energy (kinetic + potential) remains constant. Similarly, in stellar interiors, nuclear potential energy is converted to thermal and electromagnetic energy, but the total energy is conserved.

Mathematical Problem-Solving Exercises

Question 4 Energy Calculation

A comet of mass 5.0 × 1012 kg is moving at 25 km/s relative to the Sun. Calculate its kinetic energy in joules.

Use the formula for kinetic energy: KE = (1/2)mv², where m is the mass and v is the velocity. Remember to convert km/s to m/s.

Explanation:

Calculation:

First, convert the velocity from km/s to m/s:

v = 25 km/s = 25,000 m/s

Using the formula for kinetic energy: KE = (1/2)mv²

KE = (1/2) × (5.0 × 1012 kg) × (25,000 m/s)²

KE = (1/2) × (5.0 × 1012) × (6.25 × 108)

KE = 1.56 × 1021 J

This enormous amount of kinetic energy explains why cometary impacts can be so devastating. For comparison, the Chicxulub impact that likely caused the extinction of non-avian dinosaurs is estimated to have released energy equivalent to about 1023 J.

Question 5 Gravitational Potential Energy Problem

A spacecraft of mass 1,000 kg is in a circular orbit around Earth at an altitude of 400 km. Calculate the change in gravitational potential energy (in joules) if it moves to a higher orbit at 1,000 km altitude. Use G = 6.67 × 10-11 N·m2/kg2, Earth's mass = 5.97 × 1024 kg, and Earth's radius = 6,371 km.

Use the formula for gravitational potential energy: PE = -GMm/r, where G is the gravitational constant, M is Earth's mass, m is the spacecraft's mass, and r is the distance from Earth's center. Calculate the difference between the potential energy at the two altitudes.

Explanation:

Calculation:

The gravitational potential energy at a distance r from Earth's center is given by: PE = -GMm/r

For the initial orbit at 400 km altitude:

r₁ = Earth's radius + altitude = 6,371 km + 400 km = 6,771 km = 6.771 × 106 m

PE₁ = -(6.67 × 10-11) × (5.97 × 1024) × (1,000) / (6.771 × 106)

PE₁ = -5.88 × 1010 J

For the final orbit at 1,000 km altitude:

r₂ = Earth's radius + altitude = 6,371 km + 1,000 km = 7,371 km = 7.371 × 106 m

PE₂ = -(6.67 × 10-11) × (5.97 × 1024) × (1,000) / (7.371 × 106)

PE₂ = -5.40 × 1010 J

The change in potential energy is:

ΔPE = PE₂ - PE₁ = -5.40 × 1010 - (-5.88 × 1010) = 4.8 × 109 J

The positive value indicates that energy must be added to the spacecraft to move it to a higher orbit, which makes physical sense. This energy could be provided by rocket engines or other propulsion systems.

Applied Astronomy Scenarios

Question 6 Scenario Analysis

A main sequence star with 1.5 times the mass of our Sun has been observed for several decades. Spectroscopic analysis shows that hydrogen fusion is occurring in its core, converting hydrogen into helium and releasing energy.

A) Explain the primary source of the star's energy in terms of mass-energy equivalence.

B) If the star converts 4.3 × 109 kg of mass into energy each second, calculate its power output in watts. Use E = mc², where c = 3.0 × 108 m/s.

C) How does this energy production mechanism relate to the star's lifetime compared to lower-mass stars?

Explanation:

A) Source of the star's energy in terms of mass-energy equivalence:

The star's energy comes from nuclear fusion in its core, where hydrogen nuclei combine to form helium. During this process, some of the mass of the original hydrogen nuclei is converted into energy according to Einstein's mass-energy equivalence equation, E = mc². The mass of a helium nucleus is slightly less than the mass of the four hydrogen nuclei that formed it. This mass difference (called the mass defect) is converted directly into energy. This process is the primary power source for main sequence stars.

B) Power output calculation:

Using E = mc²:

Power = Energy/time = mc²/time

Power = (4.3 × 109 kg) × (3.0 × 108 m/s)²

Power = (4.3 × 109) × (9.0 × 1016)

Power = 3.87 × 1026 watts

This is approximately 10 times the luminosity of our Sun, which is about 3.8 × 1026 watts.

C) Relationship to star's lifetime:

Higher-mass stars have shorter lifetimes than lower-mass stars, despite having more hydrogen fuel. This is because higher-mass stars have much higher core temperatures and pressures, which dramatically accelerate the rate of fusion reactions. The fusion rate increases approximately with the cube of the star's mass. So while a 1.5 solar mass star has 50% more fuel than our Sun, it burns that fuel at a much faster rate, resulting in a shorter overall lifetime. This star would have a main sequence lifetime of roughly 2-3 billion years, compared to our Sun's expected 10 billion year lifetime.
